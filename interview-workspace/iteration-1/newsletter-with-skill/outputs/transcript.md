# Interview Transcript: AI Tools Newsletter for Non-Technical Managers

**Idea:** "I want to start a weekly newsletter about AI tools for non-technical managers"

---

## Phase 1: Intake

### Internal Hypothesis

The stated goal is a newsletter, but the real goal is likely one of: building an audience for future monetization, establishing thought leadership, or scratching an itch because this person sees a knowledge gap between what AI tools can do and what managers actually know. The phrasing "non-technical managers" suggests the user has experience bridging that gap -- possibly works in tech or consults with companies adopting AI.

### Gaps & Ambiguities Identified

1. No mention of monetization or business model -- is this a passion project, a lead-gen play, or a standalone business?
2. "AI tools" is enormous territory -- which vertical? Productivity? Marketing? Operations? All of them?
3. No mention of distribution channel (Substack, Beehiiv, Ghost, email list, LinkedIn)
4. No mention of existing audience or starting from zero
5. "Weekly" is a commitment -- has this person published consistently before?

### Domain Assessment

Greenfield. Non-software project (content/media). Likely solo creator, at least initially.

### Acknowledgment

A weekly newsletter translating AI tools for managers who don't have engineering backgrounds -- that's a real gap. There's a tension between covering enough ground to be useful and going deep enough to be worth reading. The positioning decisions here matter more than most people realize. Let me ask some questions to figure out what this actually looks like.

Skipped -- greenfield, non-technical project. No codebase to investigate.

## Phase 3: Interview Loop

### Round 1 — Intent & Real Goals

**Observation:** The newsletter space for AI content is crowded. What matters is whether this fills a gap the user has personally felt, or whether it's a "seems like a good idea" play.

**QUESTION:** What's happening right now that made you want to start this? Is there a specific moment -- a conversation with a manager, a frustration you keep running into -- that triggered this?

**ANSWER:** I'm a product manager at a mid-size SaaS company. I keep having the same conversation with directors and VPs across departments -- they hear about ChatGPT and Copilot and Midjourney and they either think AI will replace everyone or they think it's a toy. Neither reaction is useful. I've been sending informal emails to about 15 people internally with tool recommendations and how-to breakdowns, and three of them independently told me I should make it a real thing.

**QUESTION:** What happens if you don't build this? Those 15 people keep getting your informal emails, or does that stop too?

**ANSWER:** Honestly, the informal emails would probably taper off. I've been doing it because people asked, but without some structure I'll lose motivation. I also think the audience is way bigger than my company. Every mid-market company has this exact problem -- managers who are curious but don't know where to start.

---

### Round 2 — Audience & Positioning

**Observation:** The user has a real, validated signal from 15 internal readers. That's a strong seed. But "non-technical managers" spans everyone from a marketing director to a VP of supply chain. The more specific the audience, the more useful the newsletter.

**QUESTION:** Think about the 15 people you've been emailing. What kind of manager gets the most value from your recommendations? Give me a specific person -- their title, their department, what they were trying to do when your email helped them.

**ANSWER:** Sarah, Director of Customer Success. She manages a team of 12. She wanted to use AI to help her team write better QBR summaries and triage support tickets faster. She'd been googling around and getting overwhelmed by listicles that all said "top 10 AI tools" with no context for her situation. I walked her through three tools, explained what each one actually does in plain language, and told her which one to start with. She had her team using it within a week.

**QUESTION:** What would make someone like Sarah unsubscribe?

**ANSWER:** If it starts reading like a tech blog. If I'm explaining transformer architectures or comparing model benchmarks. She doesn't care about that. She wants to know: what can I do with this on Monday morning? If I lose that practical angle, she's out.

---

### Round 3 — Scope & Content Shape

**Observation:** The user's strength is the "here's what to do Monday morning" angle -- practical, opinionated, tool-specific. That's a clear differentiator from the AI hype newsletters. Now I need to understand what a single issue actually looks like.

**QUESTION:** Walk me through what one issue of this newsletter contains. Not the ideal future version -- what would issue #1 look like if you sat down to write it this weekend?

**ANSWER:** I'd probably pick one tool -- say, ChatGPT's custom instructions feature -- and walk through a real use case for a manager. Like: "Here's how a customer success director set up custom instructions so every prompt already knows her company's product names, key metrics, and communication style." Then I'd include the actual prompt template they could copy-paste. Maybe a short "what else I'm watching this week" section with 2-3 links and one-line takes. Probably 800-1200 words total.

**QUESTION:** Name two things readers will expect this newsletter to do that it won't.

**ANSWER:** They'll expect comprehensive tool comparisons -- like "the best AI tool for marketing" roundups. I'm not doing that, it's impossible to keep current and it's not actionable. They'll also expect me to cover enterprise AI strategy and implementation -- I'm staying at the individual and team level, not the "how should the CTO think about AI adoption" level.

---

### Round 4 — Distribution & Growth

**Observation:** Content shape is getting clear -- one deep tool walkthrough per week, practical and copy-paste ready. Now I need to understand the business side. How this reaches people matters as much as what it says.

**QUESTION:** Where are these managers already spending their attention? LinkedIn, Slack communities, industry conferences, specific podcasts? And do you have any existing audience beyond the 15 internal people -- a LinkedIn following, a Twitter presence, anything?

**ANSWER:** LinkedIn is where these people live. I have about 2,200 LinkedIn connections, mostly product and business people. I've posted maybe five times in the last year -- nothing consistent. No Twitter presence worth mentioning. I'm in a few Slack communities for product managers but nothing huge. The honest answer is I'm starting mostly from scratch on external audience.

**QUESTION:** Have you thought about which platform you'd publish on? Substack, Beehiiv, ConvertKit, something else? I'd recommend Beehiiv for this kind of newsletter -- it has better growth tools than Substack (referral programs, recommendations network, ad marketplace) and you own the email list. Substack has more built-in discovery but locks you into their ecosystem. ConvertKit is more of a raw email tool with less newsletter-specific features.

**ANSWER:** I've been assuming Substack because it's what I know, but Beehiiv sounds like the better fit if I want to grow beyond organic. Let me go with Beehiiv. I like that I own the list.

---

### Progress Check

We've got a solid picture of who this is for (mid-level managers like Sarah who want practical, Monday-morning-actionable AI guidance) and what each issue looks like (one deep walkthrough, one tool, real use cases). The main gaps are around monetization plans, publishing cadence sustainability, and how you'll measure whether this is working.

---

### Round 5 — Sustainability & Cadence

**Observation:** Weekly newsletters are a grind. The #1 killer of newsletters isn't bad content -- it's missed weeks. This person has a full-time job as a PM. I need to stress-test whether weekly is realistic.

**QUESTION:** You're a full-time PM. How many hours per week can you realistically spend on this, and what does your tool-testing workflow look like? Are you already using these tools daily, or would writing the newsletter require dedicated research time?

**ANSWER:** I use AI tools daily in my own work, so I'm constantly discovering things. The writing is what takes time. I'd estimate I can commit 3-4 hours per week -- maybe an hour of research/testing and 2-3 hours of writing and editing. I've thought about starting biweekly to be safe, but I worry about momentum.

**QUESTION:** What's your backup plan for weeks when work gets intense -- a product launch, end of quarter, vacation? Do you have a buffer issue you could pre-write, or would you skip a week?

**ANSWER:** I hadn't thought about that seriously. I could probably pre-write 2-3 "evergreen" issues that don't depend on timing -- like a walkthrough of setting up ChatGPT custom instructions, which is useful regardless of when you read it. Having a buffer of those would help. Skipping a week feels worse than sending something lighter.

---

### Round 6 — Monetization & Business Model

**Observation:** The user hasn't mentioned money once. That's either admirable focus on quality first, or it means they'll burn out doing free work. Worth probing.

**QUESTION:** What's the business model here? A few options to consider: (1) Free newsletter, monetize later through sponsorships once you hit 5-10K subscribers. (2) Freemium -- free weekly issue, paid tier with templates/prompts/deeper content. (3) Pure lead generation for consulting or a future course. (4) No monetization, just audience building. Where's your head at?

**ANSWER:** I think option 1 for now. Start free, build the audience, figure out monetization once I see what resonates. I'm not opposed to a paid tier eventually, but I don't want to split my attention early on. Sponsorships feel like the natural fit once I have enough readers -- the audience is managers with budget authority, which sponsors love. Realistically I'd like this to be generating some revenue within 12 months.

---

### Round 7 — Success Criteria & Risks

**Observation:** We're in good shape on the "what" and "who." Time to pin down what success actually looks like and what could derail this.

**QUESTION:** It's December 2025 -- you've been publishing for six months. What numbers or milestones would make you say "this is working, keep going"? And what would make you say "this isn't worth my time"?

**ANSWER:** Working: 2,000+ subscribers, 45%+ open rate, and at least a handful of inbound messages per week from readers saying the content helped them. Not working: under 500 subscribers, open rate below 30%, and it feels like shouting into the void. The qualitative signal matters more to me than the numbers honestly -- if smart people are telling me this is useful, I'll keep going even if growth is slow.

**QUESTION:** What's the thing you're most uncertain about right now?

**ANSWER:** Distribution. I know I can write good content -- the 15 internal readers proved that. What I don't know is how to get it in front of the right people consistently. LinkedIn is the obvious channel but I don't have a content strategy there. I'm worried I'll write great issues that nobody sees.

---

## Phase 4: Challenge

### Simplifier Challenge

**Observation:** The user described a pretty polished format -- deep walkthrough, prompt templates, "what I'm watching" section. That's a lot for someone doing this in 3-4 hours alongside a full-time job.

**QUESTION:** If you had to launch in 48 hours with issue #1, and it could only be one thing -- no secondary sections, no links roundup, just the core -- what's the single piece that makes it worth opening?

**ANSWER:** The tool walkthrough with the copy-paste prompt template. That's the thing nobody else does well. The "what I'm watching" section is nice-to-have but the walkthrough is the product. If someone can read my issue and immediately go use the tool better, that's the whole value prop.

### Edge Finder Challenge

**QUESTION:** Six months in, a reader emails you: "I tried the tool you recommended and it gave my team completely wrong outputs that we sent to a client." What do you do? Have you thought about the liability of recommending tools that people actually use for real work?

**ANSWER:** That's a good one. I think the framing matters -- I'm showing how I use these tools, not certifying them for anyone's specific use case. I should probably include a standard disclaimer, but more importantly, I should be honest in the newsletter about limitations. Like, "this tool is great for first drafts but always review before sending externally." If I build trust by being honest about where tools fall short, that situation is less likely. But I should think about this more carefully.

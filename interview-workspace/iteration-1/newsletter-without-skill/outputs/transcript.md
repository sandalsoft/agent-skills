# Newsletter Interview Transcript

## Starting Prompt

"I want to start a weekly newsletter about AI tools for non-technical managers"

---

## Round 1: Understanding the Audience

QUESTION: When you say "non-technical managers," who specifically are you picturing? Are these mid-level managers at Fortune 500 companies, small business owners, team leads at startups, or some other group?

ANSWER: Mostly mid-level managers at mid-to-large companies. Think someone who runs a marketing team of 8-15 people, or a project manager overseeing a product launch. They've heard about ChatGPT and maybe tried it, but they don't know what else is out there or how to actually roll these tools into their team's day-to-day work. They're not deciding company-wide AI strategy -- they just want their team to work smarter.

QUESTION: What's their biggest frustration right now when it comes to AI? Is it that they feel behind, that they've tried tools and gotten mediocre results, or something else?

ANSWER: Both, honestly. They feel like everyone's talking about AI but nobody's giving them practical advice. They've maybe tried ChatGPT for writing emails and thought "okay, that's neat but not life-changing." They don't know what they don't know. And they're scared of looking dumb -- they don't want to ask their IT department basic questions.

---

## Round 2: Content and Differentiation

QUESTION: The AI newsletter space is crowded. The Neuron, Superhuman, Ben's Bites (before it wound down), TLDR AI -- there are dozens. What would make someone open yours instead of those? What's the angle those miss?

ANSWER: Those newsletters are written for tech-curious people. They cover model releases, funding rounds, benchmark scores. My reader doesn't care about GPT-5 benchmarks. They care about "can this tool help me run my Monday standup better?" I want every issue to be something they can act on that week. Not news, not hype -- just "here's a tool, here's exactly how to use it for your job, here's what it costs."

QUESTION: So the core value prop is actionable, role-specific guidance rather than AI news. Would you cover one tool per issue in depth, or survey several?

ANSWER: I'm thinking one main tool deep-dive per issue, with maybe 2-3 quick mentions of other things. The deep-dive would walk through a real scenario -- like "You need to summarize 40 pages of customer feedback before Thursday's exec meeting. Here's how to do it with Tool X in 15 minutes." The quick mentions would be more like "also worth knowing about" for people who want to explore.

---

## Round 3: Format and Structure

QUESTION: What's your target length? And do you have a structure in mind, or is it more freeform?

ANSWER: I want it short enough to read during a morning coffee. Maybe 5-7 minutes. I'm thinking a consistent structure so people know what to expect each week. Something like: a quick intro/hook, the main tool walkthrough, the quick picks, and maybe a "one thing to try this week" call to action.

QUESTION: Would you include screenshots, video walkthroughs, or keep it text-only? And would there be a web version, or just email?

ANSWER: Definitely screenshots -- annotated ones showing exactly where to click. I think video would be great eventually but it's too much work at launch. Email-first with a web archive. I want to keep production overhead low so I can actually sustain a weekly cadence.

---

## Round 4: Voice, Tone, and Trust

QUESTION: What should it feel like to read? Casual like a friend's recommendation? Professional like a McKinsey brief? Something in between?

ANSWER: Like a smart coworker who's really into this stuff and is saving you time by filtering out the noise. Friendly but not goofy. No jargon -- if I have to use a technical term, I explain it in one sentence. I want readers to feel smarter after reading, not more confused.

QUESTION: Will you be reviewing tools honestly, including downsides? Or is this more of a recommendations-only approach?

ANSWER: Honest reviews. If a tool is overpriced or has a steep learning curve, I'll say so. That's how you build trust. I'll also be upfront about whether something has a free tier or if you need to expense it. Budget matters to these readers -- they can't just throw $50/month at every shiny tool without justification.

---

## Round 5: Business Model and Growth

QUESTION: How are you thinking about monetization? Free with sponsorships, paid subscription, freemium with a premium tier?

ANSWER: Free to start. I need to build an audience before I can charge anyone. Eventually I'd love to do a paid tier with maybe deeper guides, templates, or a community aspect. Sponsorships could work too since AI tool companies are spending a lot on marketing right now. But honestly, for the first 6 months I just want to focus on growing the list and making the content genuinely good.

QUESTION: What's your growth strategy for the first 1,000 subscribers? Do you have an existing audience or platform?

ANSWER: I have about 2,000 LinkedIn connections, mostly in product and marketing management. I'd start there -- post about each issue, share takeaways. I'd also try to get a few managers I know to forward it to their teams. Beyond that, maybe cross-promotions with other newsletters that serve managers but aren't AI-focused. I don't have a big following, so it'll be a grind at first.

---

## Round 6: Operations and Sustainability

QUESTION: How are you testing and finding tools to cover? Do you have a pipeline for discovery, or are you figuring it out issue by issue?

ANSWER: I've been bookmarking things for months. I have a running list of probably 30-40 tools I want to cover. For ongoing discovery, I follow Product Hunt, a few AI subreddits, and I'm in some Slack communities. My plan is to actually use each tool for a real task before writing about it, so I'm not just parroting marketing copy.

QUESTION: What's your realistic time commitment per issue? And what's your "this is working" metric at the 3-month mark?

ANSWER: I'm budgeting about 4-5 hours per issue. 1-2 hours testing the tool, 2 hours writing, and an hour for editing and production. At the 3-month mark, I'd feel good about 500 subscribers and a 45%+ open rate. If I'm getting replies from readers saying "I used this," that's the real signal.

---

## Round 7: Naming and Launch

QUESTION: Do you have a name in mind?

ANSWER: I've been going back and forth. Some ideas: "The Manager's AI Briefing," "Tool Sharp," "AI for the Rest of Us." Nothing feels right yet. I want something that signals practical and non-intimidating without being cheesy.

QUESTION: When do you want to launch? And do you want to do a soft launch (start publishing and iterate) or a bigger planned launch with a landing page and pre-launch list?

ANSWER: I'd like to launch in about 3 weeks. I'm leaning toward a soft launch -- put up a simple landing page, send a few LinkedIn posts, and ship Issue #1. I'd rather learn from real readers than spend a month perfecting a landing page nobody sees. I can always relaunch later once I've found the voice.
